## Overview

Lamassu (Akkadian: 𒀭𒆗 lamassu; earlier Sumerian Lamma) is a tutelary protective deity of ancient Mesopotamian religion. The figure first appears in Sumerian art (c. 2000–1800 BC) as a beneficent goddess in human form. From the Neo-Assyrian period onward, lamassu were depicted as colossal hybrid creatures with a human head (signifying intelligence), the body of a bull or lion (signifying strength) and the wings of an eagle (signifying freedom). Pairs of lamassu were carved in high relief and installed as gate-guardians at the entrances of palaces and cities; many were carved with five legs so that, viewed frontally, the creature appeared to stand still and, viewed from the side, to walk. Major surviving examples come from the Neo-Assyrian capitals at Nimrud, Khorsabad (Dur-Sharrukin) and Nineveh and are today held in the British Museum, the Louvre, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the Oriental Institute of the University of Chicago and the National Museum of Iraq. Several lamassu, including those at Nineveh and the Mosul Museum, were destroyed by Islamic State (ISIL) in 2015.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Akkadian lamassu (cuneiform 𒀭𒆗), from earlier Sumerian Lamma. The male counterpart is shedu. The compound term aladlammû was also used in Neo-Assyrian inscriptions.
- **Meaning**: 'Protective spirit' / 'tutelary deity'.
- **First use**: Attested in Sumerian art and texts from the Isin-Larsa period (c. 2000–1800 BC). The hybrid winged-bull / winged-lion form is first associated by Assyriologists with the reign of Tiglath-Pileser II (Wikipedia: Lamassu).

## Distribution

Originally an artistic and religious tradition of southern Mesopotamia (Sumer / Akkad); reaches its monumental culmination in the Neo-Assyrian Empire of the 9th–7th centuries BC, with examples from Nimrud, Khorsabad and Nineveh in present-day Iraq. The form was subsequently absorbed by the Achaemenid Persian Empire (Persepolis).

## Theories & Explanations

### Scientific

**Tutelary-deity interpretation** _(Mainstream Assyriology, British Museum, Louvre and Metropolitan Museum curators)_

Mainstream Assyriology treats the lamassu as a tutelary protective deity in Mesopotamian religion, with a documented evolution from Sumerian Lamma to Neo-Assyrian gate-guardian colossus. Royal inscriptions present the lamassu as both divine protector of the gate and material expression of royal power.
